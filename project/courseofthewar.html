The Course of the War

“You will kill ten of us, we will kill one of you, but in the end, you will tire of it first.
- Hồ Chí Minh
One of many peace protests in America

 After the North began to grow in power and sponser communist rebels from the South, the United States began its involvement in the war starting in 1961 with president John F. Kennedy's escalation plan. Part of this plan included the endorsement of Ngô Đình Diệm, the Prime Minister of the South. However, it was obvious that the only reason for Diệm's endoresment was because he was the only leader in the region opposing communism. This became apparent as the South Vietnamese lost battle after battle against the poorly equipped geurilla forces of the Việt Cộng, despite almost always having superior numbers. Diệm's ineptitude peaked in Battle of Ấp Bắc.

 The US, realizing their mistake, organized a CIA-sponsored coup in which Diệm was murdered. After President Kennedy himself was assasinated, Lyndon B. Johnson was sworn into office. He initially oppposed further involvement in the war. However, on August 2, 1964, US cruisers in the Vietnamese Bay of Tonkin were shot down. This led to the signing of the Bay of Tonkin resolution, which gave the US the right to become officially involved in the ground war.

 The war so for had relatively high American approval. However, in January of 1968, the North Vietnamese launched the Tet Offensive, a suprise attack on American and Southern forces. There were three phases, lasting from January to March. In the end, hundereds of thousands of Americans were massacred. Approval of the war in the US plummeted, pressuring Johnson to withdraw from the war. Thus, he began the process of "Vietnamization", or withdrawing US troops and training South Vietnamese troops to be more effective. This would lead to the South's downfall.
